The first question every vault asks is the right one: *do we have to give up
custody?* The answer is no. The Global Gold Standard is built so the metal stays
in approved custody and the vault remains the authoritative registrar of its own
book. Liquidity arrives not because the vault surrenders control, but because the
vault makes title legible. Those are different acts, and the distinction is the
whole point.

Most attempts to put gold on programmable rails ask the holder to trade control
for access. A bar is handed to an issuer; the issuer mints, selects custody, sets
terms, and retains freeze powers. Access is real, but so is the new dependency.
The standard takes the opposite path: control stays with the vault, and only the
*record* of title becomes something the wider market can read.

## The vault stays the registrar

Each participating vault operates its own **Title Register** — the authoritative,
on-chain book of record for the allocated bars in its custody. It is not a copy
of the vault's records and not a shadow database running alongside them. It is
the vault's book of record, written where any counterparty can verify it. The
protocol does not take possession of bars, does not take title, and does not
become the registrar.

1. The vault registers eligible bars with their identifying attributes — serial,
   refiner, weight, and fineness.
2. The Title Register reflects the current owner of record and each bar's claim
   status, giving one authoritative answer to the question every counterparty
   asks first.
3. Title events are proposed and then approved by the vault under
   Guardian-controlled policy, and each approved event is published on-chain.

Crucially, a vault can adopt the Title Register on its own — as a modernization
of its books — before a single bar is ever tokenized. Legibility comes first;
everything downstream is optional.

## Guardian-controlled policy

Authority that cannot be moved unilaterally is what makes the register
trustworthy. Title events run through Guardian-controlled policy, so no single
signer — inside the vault or outside it — can move title alone. The arrangement
is designed so that approval reflects the vault's own controls rather than any
external operator's discretion.

> The chain is the record, not the owner. The vault remains the registrar; the
> ledger simply makes that authority legible to everyone who needs to read it.

This is why the model holds up under scrutiny. An auditor, a counterparty, or a
regulator can verify the state of title without privileged access and without the
vault handing over its register. Transparency and control are not in tension
here. The vault keeps its authority precisely because the record of that
authority is open.

## Legibility, not possession

Legibility — not possession — is what unlocks liquidity. The single-issuer model
assumes the two are the same: to make gold tradable, someone must hold it and
control it. The standard separates them. Once title is legible to the wider
market under one shared rulebook — the case set out in
[the case for a shared standard](/journal/the-case-for-a-gold-standard/) — an
allocated bar can be represented, collateralized, and reach a global network
without ever leaving the vault that safeguards it.

The consequence is that quality metal no longer has to choose between staying
allocated and becoming useful. A vault's inventory can remain exactly where it
is, under exactly the controls it has always had, and still participate in a
market far larger than any single hub — which is what changes for metal that has
long sat
[outside the market](/journal/institutional-grade-gold-outside-the-market/).

## What this means for the vault

For a vault operator, the proposition is narrow on purpose. Adopt an on-chain
book of record. Keep custody, keep approval authority, keep the client
relationship. Gain a record the rest of the market can verify, and with it a path
to liquidity that was never available to stranded inventory before. The metal
does not move. What the vault can *do* with it does. Custody is not the price of
participation — it is the thing the standard is designed to protect.
