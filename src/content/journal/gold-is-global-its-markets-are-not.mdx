Gold is the most universal asset there is. A bar refined to Good Delivery
standard in one country is chemically identical to one refined in another, and
its value is recognized everywhere. The metal is global. The markets that trade
it are not.

London, Zurich, New York, Shanghai, Hong Kong, Dubai, and Singapore each
operate through their own settlement rails, approved-facility networks, custody
conventions, and liquidity centers. Each is a self-contained market with its
own trading window, its own approved-vault list, and its own definition of what
counts as deliverable. Inside a hub, gold is liquid — but only within that
hub's market structure. The universality of the metal stops at the edge of each
regional system.

## Fragmentation is the default, not the exception

The fragmentation is not an accident of any one venue. It is the predictable
result of every participant solving the same three problems independently.

- **Registration.** Each vault keeps its own authoritative book of who owns
  which bars. No two books are designed to be read by the other.
- **Representation.** Each venue has its own convention for how a bar becomes
  something tradable — a position, a balance, a receipt, a share.
- **Custody.** Each jurisdiction expresses allocated versus unallocated custody
  in its own legal and operational language.

Because there is no shared standard across these answers, moving gold from one
hub to another rarely means transferring ownership. It usually means moving the
metal: physically shipped, re-assayed, and accepted into another chain of
custody, or routed through multi-day correspondent and bullion-bank
intermediation. The asset is fungible; the infrastructure is not.

This is the same diagnosis made in
[Gold was never broken. The infrastructure underneath it was.](/journal/gold-was-never-broken/) —
the metal is sound, and the rails around it never learned to interoperate.

## The class of vaults with no pathway at all

Inside the hubs, the constraint is a matter of friction. Outside them, it is a
matter of access.

An entire class of allocated-custody facilities — state depositories, special
purpose depository institutions, private vaults, refiner inventory — sits
outside every major hub with no institutional clearing pathway whatsoever.
Clearing access, in practice, is determined less by the quality of the metal
than by the pathway attached to the vault that holds it. A bar can be
allocated, insured, audited, and held in a purpose-built facility and still be
unable to reach the market, simply because the vault is not on the right list.

> The metal is universal. The infrastructure is regional. Whether a bar can
> reach the world depends on which building it sits in.

## What fragmentation costs everyone

Shallow, walled-off liquidity is expensive even when no one names the line item.

When each market invents its own rails, liquidity pools stay thin because they
cannot pool. A buyer in one hub cannot easily reach a seller in another without
the metal physically changing hands. Spreads widen. Price discovery happens in
silos. And counterparties carry the cost of reconciling incompatible records at
every handoff — verifying purity, provenance, custody status, and title again
and again, because no prior diligence carries across the boundary.

That duplicated diligence is the quiet tax of fragmentation. The same bar is
assayed, documented, and re-verified many times over its life, not because its
quality changed, but because each new venue trusts only its own records. Work
that a shared standard would settle once is instead repeated indefinitely.

## One market, without moving the metal

The fix is not another hub. Adding a venue to a fragmented market produces a
more fragmented market. The fix is a layer that lets the existing vaults,
venues, and jurisdictions interoperate — a shared standard for how a bar is
registered, represented, and recognized that every participant can read.

Under that standard, a vault keeps its metal and remains the registrar, but
title becomes legible to the wider market. A specific bar can be represented in
a way that travels across venues without the bar itself travelling. Diligence
done once, against a common rulebook, carries everywhere. Liquidity flows to
the vaults safeguarding metal rather than pooling around whichever issuer is
easiest to trade.

The argument for that rulebook — and what specifically changes when allocated
gold finally has one — is laid out in
[the case for a shared standard](/journal/the-case-for-a-gold-standard/). The
metal was always global. With one standard underneath it, its markets can be too.
