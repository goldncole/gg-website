Ask a serious gold holder what they want and the answer is simple: real metal,
liquid markets, at scale. Ask what they actually own and the answer is almost
always a compromise on one of the three. The instruments available today force
a choice. You can hold something real, something liquid, or something scalable
— rarely all three at once.

The trade-off is so familiar it reads as a law of nature. It is not. It is an
artifact of how each product was built, and of the fact that none of them was
built on a standard the others could share.

## The three corners of the trade-off

### Real but illiquid

A physical bar in allocated custody is the genuine article. You own a specific,
identifiable piece of metal — not a claim against anyone's balance sheet. That
is the substance every other product is ultimately a proxy for. But the
mobility is limited. To use the bar in a market, you typically have to move it,
or convert it into something that is no longer the bar. The realness is total;
the liquidity is local.

### Liquid but not allocated metal

Listed gold products — funds and the shares that trade against them — solve
liquidity and institutional scale. They trade on deep markets during market
hours and slot neatly into existing portfolios. But the holder owns shares in a
trust, not a claim on a specific allocated bar. The exposure is to the gold
price, intermediated; it is not direct ownership of metal. Liquidity is real.
Allocation is not.

### Scalable but issuer-dependent

Single-issuer tokenized gold solves continuous, around-the-clock liquidity and
on-chain scale. Demand for it is proven. But minting, redemption, custody
selection, terms, and freeze powers all depend on a single issuer, and much of
the broader on-chain gold market runs on unallocated balances rather than
specific bars. The token scales; the dependency travels with it. You have
mobility, and you have a counterparty between you and the metal.

> Each product is honest about what it offers. The dishonesty is structural —
> the market presents three compromises as if one of them were the whole answer.

## Why the trade-off persisted

None of these products is a failure. Each solves exactly the problem it set out
to solve. The trilemma persists because they were built in isolation, each
inventing its own way to register a bar, represent it, and express its custody
status. With no shared standard connecting those answers, realness, liquidity,
and scale ended up living in three different instruments — and a holder could
only stand in one corner at a time.

This is the same fragmentation that keeps gold's markets walled off from one
another, examined in
[institutional-grade gold that sits outside the market](/journal/institutional-grade-gold-outside-the-market/):
quality is abundant; the pathway is not.

## What collapses the trade-off

The trade-off collapses the moment the three corners share a standard — and the
mechanism that does the collapsing is reversibility.

Picture the round trip. An allocated bar stays in a participating vault, which
remains the registrar. A reversible claim connects that specific bar to the
protocol. From eligible collateral, a fungible unit is made liquid on a
continuous global market. And — this is the part the existing products cannot
offer — the holder can travel the path in reverse, returning from the liquid
unit to allocated ownership of metal. Nothing is one-way. Nothing depends on a
single issuer's permission to mint or redeem.

That reversibility is what lets one instrument hold all three properties at
once:

- **Real**, because the bar never stops being a specific allocated bar in
  custody.
- **Liquid**, because the unit trades on a continuous global market.
- **Scalable**, because the same standard applies across participating vaults
  and jurisdictions rather than depending on one company.

The bar stays in custody. The unit moves globally. The holder chooses which end
of the round trip to stand on, and can change that choice.

The full argument for the rulebook that makes the round trip possible is in
[the case for a shared standard](/journal/the-case-for-a-gold-standard/). The
point here is narrower and, for a holder, more immediate: real, liquid, or
scalable was never a law. It was a missing standard. With one in place, the
choice stops being a choice.
