Gold has never lacked value. What it has lacked is a **shared standard** — one
agreed rulebook for registering a bar, representing it on-chain, collateralizing
it, and making it liquid without surrendering custody to a single issuer. Today
every vault, every regional market, and every jurisdiction solves those four
problems differently. The result is fragmentation: real metal, properly
allocated and audited, stranded behind operational walls that no counterparty
outside the wall can read.

The gold market is among the largest stores of value on earth, yet its plumbing
predates the systems that clear almost everything else. London spot and Zurich
Good Delivery, COMEX in New York, Shanghai, Dubai, Singapore — each is a
self-contained market with its own settlement rails and its own approved-vault
list. Cross-market access depends on correspondent banking, bullion-bank
intermediation, physical shipment, or re-assay into another chain of custody.
And outside every one of those hubs sits a further class of allocated custody —
state depositories, special-purpose depository institutions, private vaults,
refiner inventory — with no institutional clearing pathway at all.

## What fragmentation costs

When each participant invents its own rails, the costs are structural rather
than occasional. Liquidity stays shallow because it cannot pool across venues.
Counterparties carry the recurring expense of reconciling records that were
never designed to agree. A bar that is perfectly good collateral in one venue is
illegible in another, which means its owner posts it twice or not at all.

- Custody is trapped in bespoke chains of title that do not travel.
- Liquidity accrues to issuers rather than to the vaults safeguarding the metal.
- Counterparties repeat diligence that a shared standard would settle once.

The tokenized-gold market that emerged over the last decade proved the demand
for gold on programmable rails is real. It did not, however, resolve the
fragmentation. It reproduced it — each product a single issuer with sole control
over minting, redemption, custody selection, and freeze powers. Demand was
answered; the structural problem was not.

## What a standard changes

A standard does not move the metal, and it does not ask gold to become something
else. It makes the metal *legible* — to markets, to auditors, to regulators, and
to counterparties — under one set of rules that every participant can read. The
metal stays exactly where it is. What changes is who can verify it and what it
can reach.

Under the Global Gold Standard, the rulebook resolves into named layers. The
**Title Register** gives each participating vault an on-chain book of record for
the allocated bars in its custody — the authoritative answer to who holds title
to which bar. **Tokenize** lets a registered bar become a Conditional Claim
Token (CCT), an optional and reversible claim on a specific bar. **Mint**
collateralizes those claims to issue fungible Standard Metal Units (SMU) — of
which U.S. Gold (USG) is the first — backed one-for-one by locked claims rather
than by an issuer's promise. **The Reserve**, **Liquidity**, and **Security**
extend the same logic into a neutral gold unit, a market for it, and the
controls that hold the whole arrangement together. One rulebook, applied
consistently, rather than one operator standing in the middle.

### The round trip

The property that matters most is reversibility. A holder can move from an
allocated bar into liquid units and back again — registering and tokenizing a
bar, minting units against the claim, and later unwinding the claim to stand
once more in front of a specific bar in a specific vault. Title transfers to a
bankruptcy-remote collateral agent for the duration, never to an issuer, and
returns when the claim is unwound.

> Gold does not need to become something else to participate in modern markets.
> It needs a standard that lets real bars operate across a global network without
> ever leaving the vault.

This is the distinction between liquidity and surrender. In the single-issuer
model, reaching liquidity means handing control to the party that mints and can
freeze. Under a shared standard, reaching liquidity means making title legible
while custody stays put. The round trip is what keeps allocated ownership intact
on both ends of the journey — a point developed further in
[custody without compromise](/journal/custody-without-compromise/).

## Why a standard, and why now

Standards are how fragmented markets become single ones. Payments converged on
shared messaging; securities converged on shared clearing. In each case the
metal of the matter never changed — what changed was the agreement about how to
describe it, so that any participant could settle with any other. Allocated gold
has not had that agreement. It has had hubs that do not interoperate and issuers
that do not connect.

Gold was never the broken part of the system; the infrastructure around it was,
a case made in [gold was never broken](/journal/gold-was-never-broken/). A
shared standard does not repair gold. It repairs the rails — registration,
representation, collateral, and liquidity, written once and read everywhere — so
that a bar in one jurisdiction can reach a global network without leaving the
vault that holds it. The metal has always been ready. The standard is what lets
it travel.
