Gold has never lacked value. What it has lacked is a **shared standard** — one
agreed way to register a bar, represent it on-chain, collateralize it, and make
it liquid without surrendering custody to a single issuer. Today every vault,
every market, and every jurisdiction solves these problems differently, and the
result is fragmentation that strands real metal behind operational walls.

## What fragmentation costs

When each participant invents its own rails, liquidity pools stay shallow and
counterparties carry the cost of reconciling incompatible records. A bar that is
perfectly good collateral in one venue is illegible in another.

- Custody is trapped in bespoke chains of title.
- Liquidity flows to issuers rather than to the vaults safeguarding metal.
- Counterparties duplicate diligence that a shared standard would settle once.

## What a standard changes

A standard does not move the metal. It makes the metal *legible* — to markets,
to regulators, and to counterparties — under one set of rules that everyone can
read.

### The round trip

The key property is reversibility. A holder can move from an allocated bar into
liquid units and back again, accessing market liquidity without giving up the
right to allocated ownership.

> Gold does not need to become something else to participate in modern markets.
> It needs a standard that lets real bars operate across a global network.
